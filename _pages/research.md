My research examines how institutional design shapes educational opportunity. I study how policies, information, and organizational decisions influence educational and labor market outcomes using experimental, quasi-experimental, and mixed methods. While my work spans higher education, vocational education and training, and labor market policy, the common thread is understanding how institutions shape individual decision-making and long-run outcomes.

## Current Projects

### Unequal and Persistent Effects of Student Loan Policy

*with Alex Monday*

**Status:** Working paper (available [here](https://edworkingpapers.com/ai26-1483))

Why do uniform policies produce unequal effects across colleges? This paper examines two reforms to the federal Parent PLUS loan program to understand how institutions' financial dependence on student borrowing shapes their responses to policy change.

Using a panel of U.S. colleges from 2006–2023, we show that Historically Black Colleges and Universities (HBCUs), which rely more heavily on Parent PLUS borrowing, experienced substantially larger declines in borrowing and enrollment after a tightening of federal credit standards. Although borrowing recovered after the policy was reversed, enrollment did not fully rebound. Institutions responded by expanding admissions while reducing spending on instruction and student services, suggesting that temporary policy shocks can generate persistent institutional consequences.

More broadly, the study shows how seemingly uniform higher education policies can generate lasting inequalities when institutions differ in their underlying financial vulnerability.

### Fast Track to Success? A Mixed Methods Evaluation of Condensed Course Formats at Tennessee Community Colleges

*with Kaylee T. Matheny, Gus Gluek, Rachel Baker, Eric Bettinger, Alex Monday, and Hidahis Mesa*

**Status:** Revise and resubmit, Journal of Policy Analysis and Management ([working paper](https://edworkingpapers.com/ai26-1483) and [replication package](https://dataverse.harvard.edu/dataset.xhtml?persistentId=doi:10.7910/DVN/ZQBEH5) available)

As colleges search for ways to improve student success, many are replacing traditional 15-week semesters with shorter, more intensive course formats. This paper examines whether condensed courses improve academic outcomes using administrative data from every public community college in Tennessee alongside interviews with students, faculty, and staff.

Combining quantitative and qualitative evidence, we find that condensed courses substantially improve course completion, grades, and withdrawal rates while also revealing important tradeoffs related to student stress, knowledge retention, and opportunities to build relationships with instructors and peers.

Together, these findings highlight both the promise and the practical challenges of redesigning academic calendars to improve student success.

### Choice Architecture in Occupational Choices

*with Enzo Brox, Patricia Palffy, Claudio Schilter, and Uschi Backes-Gellner*

**Status:** Working paper (available [here](https://ideas.repec.org/p/iso/educat/0255.html))

How does the design of online platforms shape young people's career choices? This paper studies how adolescents respond to the way occupational recommendations are ranked and presented on Yousty, Switzerland's largest online platform for apprenticeship search.

Using detailed platform data and quasi-experimental variation in the presentation of recommendations, we examine how choice architecture influences occupational search and application behavior. We find that even small design features affect which occupations students explore, save, and ultimately apply to.

The results suggest that seemingly minor design choices on digital platforms can meaningfully influence education-to-work transitions by shaping the opportunities young people consider and pursue.

### The Effect of Postsecondary Educational Institutions on Local Economies: A Bird's-Eye View

*with Patrick Lehnert, Uschi Backes-Gellner, and Eric Bettinger*

**Status:** Under review (working paper available [here](https://www.nber.org/papers/w32679))

How do colleges shape the economic vitality of the communities they serve? While governments frequently invest in expanding higher education to promote regional development, surprisingly little evidence exists on whether these investments stimulate local economic growth.

This paper combines newly assembled historical data on public college branch campus openings with a novel satellite-based measure of local economic activity to estimate the economic effects of branch campuses in Tennessee and Texas. The approach allows us to examine local impacts at a much finer geographic scale than has previously been possible.

We find that opening a branch campus increases local economic activity and contributes to higher educational attainment and employment in fields aligned with newly offered programs. These findings provide new evidence that investments in higher education can strengthen regional human capital and contribute to local economic development.
